Executive Summary

  • Establish a sovereign, publicly backed Nordic AI infrastructure to ensure European digital autonomy, transparency, and ethical governance.
  • Leverage abundant renewable energy and existing industrial assets to build sustainable, cost-effective AI compute centers across the Nordic region.
  • Integrate leading universities and launch a Nordic AI Visa program to attract, retain, and develop world-class AI talent.
  • Prioritize open science, democratic oversight, and citizen engagement to set new global standards for trustworthy and accountable AI.
  • Reinvest AI-generated revenues into social innovation, including Universal Basic Income pilots, to ensure broad societal benefit and resilience against labor market disruption.

The Nordic AI Initiative represents a transformative opportunity to establish European leadership in ethical, sovereign artificial intelligence. This proposal outlines the creation of a publicly backed AI infrastructure platform, anchored in Sweden with broader Nordic cooperation, designed to provide a trusted, transparent alternative to private U.S. and Chinese AI systems.

The initiative addresses three critical challenges: growing geopolitical instability around AI development, the concentration of AI capabilities in unaccountable private entities, and the absence of AI infrastructure aligned with European values of transparency, sustainability, and democratic governance.

Economic Opportunity: Conservative projections indicate potential revenues of €1B–3.5B annually by Year 5, scaling to €6.5B–14.5B by Year 10, with Sweden's public ownership potentially capturing €2B–6B annually in long-term value.

Strategic Timing: Recent developments including Northvolt's bankruptcy, growing concerns about AI safety and accountability, and Sweden's commitment to AI leadership create an unprecedented window for this initiative.

Political Urgency

We’re falling behind in the global AI race. Without decisive and substantial investment now, it will soon be too late to regain leadership. By 2030, AI will be integral to education, government, and society — but if we don’t act, those critical systems will be designed and controlled by foreign powers. The values shaping these technologies will reflect others’ priorities, not our own. The window to secure Nordic AI sovereignty is closing rapidly. If we fail to invest boldly today, we risk becoming dependent on external actors with little influence over the future of our digital infrastructure.

At first glance, investing €10 billion, or even €5 billion in sovereign AI infrastructure might seem extreme. But what seems expensive today will seem cheap in hindsight — because the cost of catching up to existing AI powers grows exponentially over time.

1. Strategic Context and Vision

1.1 Core Vision

The Nordic AI Initiative aims to establish a publicly backed AI infrastructure platform that represents Europe’s strategic response to the dominance of private AI monopolies. Unlike corporate AI systems that are primarily designed to maximize profits, this initiative emphasizes a different set of values. It seeks to build AI systems grounded in democratic accountability, ensuring that governance is transparent and subject to public oversight. Ethical alignment is a cornerstone, meaning that AI technologies should serve the broader societal good rather than narrow private interests.

Sustainability is another key priority. The platform is envisioned to operate in a carbon-neutral manner, leveraging the abundant renewable energy sources available in the Nordic countries. Sovereignty also plays a critical role: Europe must maintain control over its critical AI infrastructure and data to safeguard its autonomy in this strategically vital area. Finally, transparency is essential, with an open science approach that supports the use of auditable models and datasets.

The fundamental premise behind this initiative is reminiscent of Europe’s historic achievements in strategic technology sectors — just as Airbus was developed to compete with Boeing, and CERN was created to advance fundamental physics research, the Nordic AI Initiative aims to build Europe’s independent capability in one of the most important technologies of the 21st century.

1.2 Strategic Rationale

Geopolitical Imperatives

Currently, the AI ecosystem is largely controlled by a few powerful private companies, mostly headquartered in the United States, with China also rapidly expanding its capabilities. This concentration raises several concerns for Europe. First, there is a democratic deficit: critical infrastructure is effectively governed by unelected private entities, which undermines public accountability. Second, dependence on foreign AI systems creates geopolitical vulnerabilities, as Europe's access to these technologies could be disrupted or influenced by external actors.

Moreover, the flow of profits generated from European data and markets largely benefits foreign shareholders, leading to a value extraction problem. Finally, there is a risk that AI systems are optimized to prioritize user engagement and profit maximization rather than the broader societal benefit, which can lead to misaligned incentives and undesirable outcomes.

Nordic Competitive Advantages

The Nordic region offers a unique combination of strengths that make it especially well-suited to lead ethical AI development. One of the most significant assets is trust. Nordic countries consistently rank at the top in global indices measuring trust, transparency, and governance. This high-trust social fabric supports democratic AI governance and provides a stable foundation for long-term stewardship.

Institutional stability is another advantage, with robust democratic institutions that have proven their ability to manage complex societal challenges over time. The Nordic countries have also demonstrated regulatory leadership, as exemplified by initiatives like GDPR, setting global standards for technology governance and digital rights.

On the technical and economic front, the Nordics boast abundant clean energy resources — including hydroelectric, wind, and geothermal power — that are ideal for powering energy-intensive AI infrastructure sustainably. The region’s digital infrastructure is among the most advanced worldwide, with excellent broadband connectivity and state-of-the-art data centers.

Additionally, the Nordic region benefits from world-class universities and research institutions, such as KTH, Chalmers, Lund, and Uppsala, which provide a strong foundation for cutting-edge AI research and talent development. There is also a vibrant innovation ecosystem characterized by close collaboration between public and private sectors, fostering technological advancements and enabling rapid commercialization.

2. Foundational Framework

2.1 Core Pillars of the Proposal

Governance and Ownership Structure

The Nordic AI Initiative would operate under a government-backed foundation or sovereign fund structure designed to balance public accountability with operational independence. This hybrid governance model draws inspiration from successful Nordic institutions, such as Sweden’s AP pension funds, which benefit from strong insulation against political cycles. Public accountability is maintained through regular parliamentary oversight and citizen advisory boards, ensuring that decisions remain transparent and inclusive. Additionally, the initiative is anchored in Nordic cooperation frameworks, fostering collaborative governance across the region, while aligning with broader European digital sovereignty goals and funding mechanisms.

Infrastructure Investment Strategy

Prioritizing sustainability, the initiative focuses on developing green compute infrastructure throughout Nordic territories. Northern Sweden and Norway, with their abundant hydropower, wind resources, and naturally cool climates, are prime locations for energy-efficient data centers. The infrastructure will be designed with a distributed architecture in mind, connecting regional data centers through high-speed networks to maximize resilience and performance. Innovative sustainability measures like waste heat recovery will be integrated to supply district heating systems, reducing environmental impact. The entire system will be modular and scalable, allowing capacity to grow dynamically with demand.

Academic and Research Integration

The Nordic AI Initiative is deeply integrated with leading universities, creating a rich talent pipeline and a rigorous research validation ecosystem. Institutions such as KTH Royal Institute of Technology spearhead AI and computer science research, while Lund University contributes expertise in advanced materials and computing infrastructure, notably through the MAX IV synchrotron. Uppsala University brings strengths in computational biology and interdisciplinary applications, complemented by Chalmers University’s focus on industrial AI and engineering excellence. To support this ecosystem, shared compute resources will be made available for academic research and PhD training, fostering innovation and collaboration.

Global Talent Attraction

To attract world-class AI researchers, the initiative introduces innovative citizenship and residency pathways. The Swedish AI Visa Program exemplifies this effort, offering fast-track permanent residency for AI researchers, engineers, and safety experts. The program also ensures comprehensive family integration support, covering education and healthcare needs for relocating families. By providing a clear pathway to Swedish and Nordic citizenship, it encourages long-term commitment from top talent. This approach offers a compelling alternative to restrictive visa systems in other countries and addresses the challenges posed by geopolitical instability worldwide.

2.2 Governance and Trust Framework

Transparency and Accountability

The Nordic AI Initiative aims to set new benchmarks for transparency in AI development. It embraces an open science approach, making model weights, training datasets, and alignment protocols fully auditable. Public reporting will be a regular practice, with detailed disclosures about AI capabilities, limitations, and safety assessments. Oversight will be ensured by parliamentary committees staffed with technical experts, providing ongoing democratic supervision. Furthermore, citizen participation will be encouraged through public consultations before major model deployments, fostering trust and inclusiveness.

Ethical AI Development

Ethical considerations are embedded throughout the initiative’s processes. An independent Ethics Board, comprising philosophers, technologists, and citizen representatives, will oversee AI development to ensure alignment with societal values. Dedicated research teams will focus on AI safety and value alignment, while systematic efforts will be made to identify and mitigate algorithmic bias. Ultimately, the AI systems developed will be designed to support and strengthen democratic institutions rather than undermine them, reflecting the core democratic values of the Nordic region.

3. Energy Infrastructure and Strategic Positioning

3.1 Green Compute Infrastructure Development

Why Energy Infrastructure Is Central to AI Leadership

Modern AI development is fundamentally limited by access to reliable and affordable energy. Large language models and other advanced AI systems demand immense computational power, not only for training but also for ongoing inference. Unlike many traditional industries, AI's energy consumption is continuous and predictable, requiring 24/7 operation with consistent baseload demand. Its energy needs scale linearly with model complexity, making it highly scalable. The infrastructure can be located flexibly wherever there is sufficient power and cooling capacity. Additionally, AI centers generate significant waste heat, presenting opportunities to integrate with district heating systems and improve overall energy efficiency.

Strategic Insight: Countries that control access to green, cost-effective energy infrastructure gain a sustainable competitive edge in AI. Leveraging abundant renewable resources enables delivery of AI services at lower cost while honoring carbon neutrality commitments.

Nordic Energy Advantages

Northern Sweden and Norway stand out as ideal locations for sustainable AI infrastructure development due to their exceptional renewable energy resources and favorable climate:

Resource Capacity AI Infrastructure Benefits
Hydroelectric Power 65+ TWh annually (Sweden) Stable baseload, low marginal cost, carbon-free
Wind Power 27+ TWh annually (Sweden) Complementary generation profile, scalable
Natural Cooling Sub-zero temperatures 6+ months Reduced cooling infrastructure costs
District Heating 50% of Swedish heating demand Waste heat monetization opportunity

Infrastructure Development Plan

The Nordic AI Initiative proposes establishing a distributed network of green compute centers strategically placed across the region. The primary hub, located in the Skellefteå/Luleå area, will host large-scale training facilities that leverage the region’s existing data center ecosystem. Secondary hubs in Västerås and Borlänge will focus on inference and application-specific computing needs. Additionally, close integration with university research campuses such as KTH, Chalmers, Lund, and Uppsala will foster innovation. Cross-border expansion is planned to incorporate Norway’s hydropower and Denmark’s wind resources, maximizing renewable energy usage.

3.2 Strategic Repurposing of Industrial Assets

The Northvolt Opportunity

Although Northvolt’s recent bankruptcy was a setback for battery manufacturing, it opens up a unique opportunity to repurpose their facilities for AI infrastructure. These sites are well-suited for high-density compute workloads due to their industrial design and power capabilities:

Transferable Assets

  • Industrial Facilities: Spacious buildings engineered for precision manufacturing
  • Power Infrastructure: Robust grid connections capable of megawatt-scale loads
  • Clean Room Capabilities: Controlled environments ideal for sensitive electronics
  • Local Workforce: Skilled technical personnel with advanced manufacturing experience
  • Logistics Networks: Established transportation and supply chain systems
  • Government Relationships: Strong ties with municipal and regional authorities

Conversion Strategy

Transforming Northvolt’s facilities into AI compute centers offers several key advantages. The existing buildings and infrastructure enable an accelerated timeline, cutting deployment time by 12 to 18 months. Acquiring these distressed assets is also cost-efficient, often available at a fraction of the cost of new builds. Maintaining operations in these sites supports community continuity, preserving jobs and economic vitality in the regions. Moreover, these locations have already been vetted for large-scale industrial use, reducing risk and regulatory hurdles.

Long-term Energy Strategy

The AI infrastructure will be powered entirely by a diverse portfolio of renewable energy sources. Hydropower will provide a reliable baseload through long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) with Nordic utilities like Vattenfall. Wind power will offer flexible generation to balance variable compute demand. Complementing this will be advanced energy storage solutions—including batteries and hydrogen storage—for grid stability and peak load management. Waste heat recovery systems will integrate with district heating networks to enhance sustainability and efficiency. Looking ahead, partnerships in Small Modular Reactor (SMR) research aim to explore low-carbon expansion options beyond 2035.

4. Products, Services and Market Opportunity

4.1 Core Product Portfolio

Public Sector AI Services

The Nordic AI Initiative’s core mission centers on delivering AI applications that enhance democratic governance and improve public service efficiency:

Government and Administrative Applications

  • Policy Analysis and Drafting: AI-powered analysis of proposed legislation, regulatory impact assessments, and automated generation of policy documents
  • Legal System Support: Tools for case law research, legal document summarization, and judicial decision support
  • Citizen Service Automation: Multilingual chatbots, automated form processing, and real-time application status tracking to streamline government services
  • Regulatory Compliance: Automated monitoring and reporting to ensure adherence to regulations

Healthcare and Social Services

  • Clinical Decision Support: Evidence-based treatment recommendations and diagnostic assistance tools for healthcare providers
  • Administrative Efficiency: Optimization of scheduling, resource allocation, and patient flow management
  • Public Health Monitoring: Disease surveillance, outbreak detection, and health trend analysis capabilities
  • Social Services Optimization: AI-driven case management, benefits allocation, and intervention targeting

Education and Research

  • Personalized Learning: Adaptive educational content and assessment tailored to individual student needs
  • Research Acceleration: Tools for scientific literature review, hypothesis generation, and research methodology support
  • Language Preservation: AI applications supporting Nordic and minority language maintenance and translation

Commercial and Enterprise Services

To support its public mission while establishing commercial viability, the initiative will offer revenue-generating services, including:

  • API-Based AI Services: Standardized interfaces allowing Nordic businesses to easily integrate AI capabilities
  • Secure Cloud Infrastructure: GDPR-compliant data hosting and processing tailored for European enterprises
  • Industry-Specific Solutions: Customized AI models addressing needs in manufacturing, logistics, finance, and telecommunications
  • Consulting and Implementation: Expert guidance for AI adoption and digital transformation projects

International and Strategic Services

The Nordic AI Initiative will position itself as a trusted global alternative to dominant U.S. and Chinese AI systems by offering:

  • EU Member State Licensing: White-label AI services tailored for European governments
  • Democratic Partner Nations: AI infrastructure for countries seeking non-authoritarian technology partners
  • International Organizations: AI services supporting UN agencies, NGOs, and multilateral institutions
  • Academic and Research Networks: Compute resources and AI tools to facilitate global research collaboration

A robust financial plan is essential to ensure the Nordic AI Initiative’s long-term viability and credibility. This section provides a more granular breakdown of costs, clarifies funding sources, and addresses market competition.

  • CAPEX: Includes data center construction, hardware acquisition, and facility upgrades (e.g., Northvolt conversion).
  • OPEX: Covers salaries, energy costs, ongoing maintenance, cybersecurity, and operational support.
  • R&D: Encompasses foundational model development, university partnerships, and upskilling programs.
  • Marketing/Legal: Includes outreach, regulatory compliance, and partnership management.

Investment Returns Timeline: Initial revenue generation is expected by Year 2-3 (pilot services), with break-even projected by Year 6-7 under conservative scenarios. Full ROI is targeted within 10 years, with positive cash flow from Year 5 onward.

Funding Sources and Model

  • Public Funding: The majority of capital will be provided by Swedish and Nordic governments, leveraging sovereign wealth funds and dedicated public investment vehicles.
  • EU Grants and Subsidies: Significant support will be sought from EU programs (e.g., Horizon Europe, Digital Europe Programme) and green transition funds, especially for infrastructure and R&D.
  • Private Partnerships: Private sector involvement will be limited to non-controlling, minority stakes in specific infrastructure or service partnerships (e.g., cloud integration, specialized hardware), ensuring public control is maintained.
  • Phased Funding Approach: Each phase will have clear investment triggers and milestones, with subsequent funding contingent on meeting performance and governance benchmarks.

Contingency Planning: A reserve fund equivalent to 12 months of core OPEX will be maintained. In the event of revenue shortfalls, phased investment and cost controls will be enacted, with emergency public funding as a last resort.

Market Competition Analysis

  • Competitive Differentiators: Nordic AI will compete on trust, transparency, and compliance with European values—offering a “sovereignty premium” for governments and enterprises seeking alternatives to U.S./Chinese providers.
  • Cost Competitiveness: Access to abundant renewable energy and repurposed industrial assets enables lower operational costs, allowing competitive pricing for both public and commercial clients.
  • Strategic Partnerships: Early alliances with EU institutions, public sector agencies, and Nordic industry will secure anchor customers and reduce reliance on volatile commercial markets.
  • Innovation and Niche Focus: By prioritizing multilingual, public sector, and regulated industry applications, Nordic AI can serve markets underserved by global tech giants.
  • Risk Management: Ongoing market analysis and scenario planning will ensure the initiative can adapt to aggressive pricing, new entrants, or shifts in global AI regulation.

5. Talent Strategy and Global Positioning

5.1 Global and Local Talent Strategy

The Nordic AI Visa Program

A key pillar of the initiative’s success lies in attracting world-class AI talent from around the globe. The Nordic AI Visa Program offers unprecedented incentives to AI researchers, engineers, and safety experts to relocate to the broader Nordic region:

Fast-Track Naturalization Pathways

  • Expedited Residency: Permanent residency granted within 12 months for qualifying AI professionals
  • Citizenship Track: Path to citizenship in Nordic countries reduced to 3-5 years (compared to the standard 5-7 years)
  • Family Integration: Comprehensive support including spousal work authorization and education for children
  • Nordic Mobility: Cross-border work permits enabling employment across Nordic countries

Qualifying Criteria

  • Technical Expertise: Advanced degree in AI/ML or equivalent professional experience
  • Publication or Project Record: Demonstrated contributions to open research or socially beneficial AI projects
  • Commitment to Public Service: Multi-year commitment to projects under the Nordic AI Initiative
  • Ethical Alignment: Proven dedication to responsible AI development

Competitive Positioning

This program addresses critical shortcomings in current immigration frameworks:

  • U.S. Visa Uncertainty: Avoids the H-1B lottery and lengthy green card processes
  • Quality of Life: Attracts families with Nordic work-life balance, healthcare, and education
  • Political Stability: Benefits from predictable governance and consistent policy support
  • Mission Alignment: Enables work on socially beneficial AI rather than profit-driven projects
  • Research Freedom: Supports open science with unrestricted publication and collaboration

Strengthening the Nordic AI Talent Pipeline

The initiative prioritizes cultivating and expanding local AI talent to build a sustainable workforce. Strategic partnerships with universities, polytechnics, and vocational institutions across the Nordic countries will enhance AI-focused curricula from secondary education through advanced research programs. Dedicated funding will support new degree tracks, interdisciplinary learning, and practical research opportunities.

Community outreach initiatives, coding bootcamps, online courses, and workshops will democratize AI literacy and ensure broad access to foundational skills. Upskilling and reskilling programs will assist workers in sectors impacted by AI-driven automation, offering subsidized training, career counseling, and placement services for transitions into AI-related roles such as data science, ML operations, and AI ethics.

University and Research Institution Collaboration

Strong collaboration across a network of Nordic academic and research institutions will underpin talent development and innovation. Key partners include:

  • KTH Royal Institute of Technology: Joint faculty appointments, funded doctoral positions, and shared AI research infrastructure.
  • Chalmers University of Technology: Focus on industrial AI applications and engineering systems.
  • Lund University: Expertise in computational infrastructure and high-performance computing.
  • Uppsala University: Interdisciplinary AI research, ethics, and social impact.
  • University of Helsinki: Strength in machine learning theory and data science.
  • Aalto University: Innovation and entrepreneurship in AI technologies.

International partnerships with leading institutions such as MIT, Stanford, and Oxford will complement Nordic efforts through exchange programs and collaborative research.

Retention Strategies and Quality of Life

Retaining top talent requires more than competitive compensation. The Nordic AI Initiative will offer clear career pathways, mentorship, research sabbaticals, and leadership development opportunities. Employees will benefit from the region’s renowned quality of life—including work-life balance, parental leave, universal healthcare, and world-class education for families. Flexible work arrangements, support for remote work, and relocation assistance will further enhance retention. A mission-driven culture emphasizing public service and societal impact will foster long-term commitment among both local and international talent.

Key Deliverables: Operational AI services, proven government applications, expanded infrastructure

Investment Required: €1B in infrastructure expansion and operations

Revenue Target: €50M-200M from pilot services

Phase 4: EU Scaling and Commercial Launch (Years 3-5)

Objective: Scale across EU member states and launch commercial services

  • EU Government Contracts: Secure AI service contracts with multiple European governments

  • Infrastructure Completion: Full distributed compute network operational across Nordic region
  • International Partnerships: Strategic alliances with democratic nations seeking AI alternatives
  • Research Leadership: Establish Nordic AI Initiative as leading voice in AI ethics and governance

Key Deliverables: Multi-country deployments, commercial revenue growth, thought leadership

Investment Required: €2.5B in expansion and product development

Revenue Target: €1B-3.5B annual revenue by Year 5

Phase 5: Global Positioning (Years 5-10)

Objective: Establish as global alternative to U.S./Chinese AI systems

  • Global Service Launch: AI infrastructure services for democratic nations worldwide
  • Next-Generation Technology: Advanced AI systems incorporating latest research breakthroughs
  • Sustainability Leadership: Carbon-negative operations through advanced heat recovery and renewable energy
  • Democratic AI Standards: Lead international efforts to establish governance standards for national AI systems
  • Economic Impact: Become significant contributor to Swedish and Nordic GDP
  • Ecosystem Development: Catalyst for broader Nordic AI industry and startup ecosystem

Key Deliverables: Global market presence, technology leadership, substantial economic impact

Investment Required: €2B in global expansion and advanced R&D

Revenue Target: €6.5B-14.5B annual revenue by Year 10

6.2 Risk Management and Mitigation Strategies

Technical Risks

  • Talent Competition: Private companies offering higher compensation
    Mitigation: Mission-driven culture, work-life balance, citizenship pathways
  • Infrastructure Challenges: Scaling compute infrastructure efficiently
    Mitigation: Phased deployment, partnerships with established cloud providers

Economic and Political Risks

  • Funding Shortfalls: Insufficient government or institutional investment
    Mitigation: Diversified funding sources, revenue generation from early phases
  • Political Changes: Shifts in government priorities or Nordic cooperation
    Mitigation: Cross-party consensus building, institutionalized governance structure
  • Market Competition: Aggressive pricing from established AI providers
    Mitigation: Value differentiation through trust, transparency, and specialization

Regulatory and Geopolitical Risks

  • EU Regulatory Changes: Evolving AI regulation affecting operations
    Mitigation: Active participation in regulatory development, compliance-by-design
  • Trade Restrictions: Export controls on AI technology or hardware
    Mitigation: European supply chains, technology independence
  • Geopolitical Tensions: Pressure from U.S. or Chinese governments
    Mitigation: Strong EU institutional support, democratic legitimacy

7. Conclusion and Next Steps

7.1 Strategic Imperative

The Nordic AI Initiative is not merely a promising economic prospect—it is a strategic necessity for preserving European sovereignty in a rapidly digitizing world. As artificial intelligence becomes central to global competitiveness, national security, and the very fabric of democratic governance, Europe faces growing risks from the over-concentration of AI capabilities in a few dominant private actors. These monopolies not only constrain innovation but challenge the autonomy of democratic societies.

The window for action is narrowing. Early leadership in AI infrastructure creates advantages that are hard to overcome later. If Europe waits for market forces alone to provide alternatives to private monopolies, it will likely find itself outpaced. Deliberate public investment is needed to create AI infrastructure that is transparent, trusted, and aligned with democratic principles.

7.2 Unique Nordic Advantages

The Nordic region is uniquely positioned to lead this initiative, thanks to a rare combination of structural strengths. Globally recognized for ethical governance and public trust, the region also benefits from abundant renewable energy sources—crucial for powering sustainable AI operations. Its universities and tech sector provide deep technical talent, while sovereign wealth funds and stable public finances offer a strong financial foundation.

In addition, political stability and mature democratic institutions make the Nordics well-suited to steward a long-term, mission-driven AI effort. Finally, the region enjoys credibility on the world stage, allowing it to champion democratic values in global AI governance.

7.3 Transformative Potential

The success of this initiative would ripple far beyond the Nordic region, influencing the future of Europe, global democracy, and technological progress.

For Europe

A thriving Nordic AI effort could prove that democratically governed technology can rival or surpass authoritarian and corporate models. It would offer EU member states a trusted alternative to foreign AI systems, while advancing Europe's leadership in AI ethics and governance. Beyond trust, this initiative could generate high-value jobs and catalyze growth in next-generation technologies.

For Global Democracy

The initiative would help reduce global dependence on AI systems developed under authoritarian or opaque conditions. It could set new standards for accountable AI development and deployment, empower democratic institutions with AI-enhanced tools, and act as a global counterweight to surveillance-oriented technologies.

For Technological Progress

With a focus on openness and safety, the initiative would support cutting-edge research in alignment and responsible AI. It could serve as a testbed for sustainable AI infrastructure and pioneer democratic models of technology governance that others can emulate.

Leveraging AI Revenues for Social Resilience: A Path Toward Universal Basic Income (UBI)

As artificial intelligence transforms the labor market, certain sectors and professions will inevitably face disruption and job displacement. The Nordic AI Initiative recognizes both the risks and opportunities of this transition. To ensure that the benefits of AI-driven productivity gains are shared equitably across society, the initiative proposes that a portion of future revenues generated by sovereign Nordic AI infrastructure be allocated to social innovation—most notably, the gradual introduction of a Universal Basic Income (UBI).

This approach would begin with targeted UBI pilot programs in regions or sectors most affected by automation, providing a financial safety net for workers transitioning to new roles or pursuing retraining. Over time, as AI-generated revenues grow, the scope of UBI could be expanded, helping to offset income losses, reduce inequality, and support lifelong learning. By reinvesting AI profits into social resilience, the Nordics can set a global example of how advanced technology can strengthen—rather than erode—social solidarity and economic security.

This vision aligns with core Nordic values of inclusion and fairness, ensuring that technological progress benefits all citizens and that no one is left behind in the AI era.

7.4 Call to Action

To seize this opportunity and mitigate the growing risks, immediate steps are required at the national, regional, and EU levels.

For the Swedish Government

Sweden should establish a high-level task force, bringing together leaders from ministries, academia, and the tech sector. An initial allocation of €50 million in the 2025 budget would enable a feasibility study and early-stage planning. Engaging regional partners through the Nordic Council, and evaluating the potential repurposing of Northvolt assets for AI infrastructure, would help accelerate progress.

For Nordic Partners

A coordinated Nordic effort is essential. This includes aligning energy policies to support cross-border renewable initiatives, harmonizing immigration frameworks to facilitate a Nordic AI Visa program, and pooling research infrastructure across universities. Presenting a unified Nordic voice in EU-level AI policy discussions will amplify impact.

For EU Institutions

European institutions must recognize the Nordic AI Initiative as central to digital sovereignty. This includes incorporating it into broader EU programs, ensuring regulatory alignment with the AI Act, and co-funding through existing investment vehicles. Recognizing AI infrastructure as a pillar of European strategic autonomy is not just desirable—it is imperative.

7.5 Historical Parallel and Vision

This initiative builds on a long tradition of ambitious European collaborations—CERN, Airbus, and the European Space Agency among them—that successfully combined technological excellence with public purpose. Like these, the Nordic AI Initiative could affirm Europe’s ability to lead in critical technologies without compromising democratic values.

Nordic leaders now face a pivotal decision: remain dependent on foreign, privately controlled AI systems, or invest in sovereign capabilities designed to serve democratic interests. This initiative provides a clear path toward autonomy, innovation, and leadership in what is arguably the defining technology of the century.

The Vision: AI as Public Infrastructure

By 2035, the Nordic AI Initiative could form the backbone of European digital governance, offering trusted AI services to hundreds of millions of citizens. The economic potential is enormous—billions in value generation, thousands of jobs—yet the true prize lies in showing the world that artificial intelligence can strengthen democracy, not undermine it.

This is not just an economic opportunity—it is a historic obligation to future generations, who deserve AI systems designed to serve, not exploit, their societies.
